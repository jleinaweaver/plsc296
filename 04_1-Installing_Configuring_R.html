Using R for Vectors of Data

# Save a list of numbers as the object 'x1'
x1 <- c(64, 57, 52, 58, 67)

# Print the numbers in the object
x1

One of the remarkably powerful uses of R is that it will let you save lists of numbers (e.g. data) using a named object.

Chapter Notes

Nominal-Level Variables

  • Nominal variables are the least precise.
  • A nominal-level variable communicates differences between units of analysis on the characteristics being measured.
  • The values are mutually exclusive and completely exhaustive.
  • Some nominal variables are binary.
  • Gender is a good example

Ordinal-Level Variables

  • Variables measured at the ordinal level may be rank ordered. That is, someone or something may have more or less of the characteristic being measured by the variable.
  • Think about the level of education individuals have completed. One may have completed an advanced graduate degree while another may have dropped out of high school. The one with the graduate degree has more education than the one who dropped out of high school.

Interval-Level Variables

  • Interval-level variables provide precise measurements of a characteristic, such as age or income, or any other characteristic, which can be measured along a continuous spectrum.
  • Some interval-level variables have discrete values or continuous values.
    • Discrete values usually count the number of times something has occurred.
    • Continuous values can have an infinite number of unique values and can be precisely estimated with decimal places
